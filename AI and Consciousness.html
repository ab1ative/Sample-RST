AI is not conscious

And it's not on me to prove otherwise; it's on those making ridiculous claims to back them up.

By Matthew Manno, Ph.D.  ·  July 20, 2026

AI is a tool like any other. It has valid uses for research, for example. Google is not adept at answering vague questions and finding sources the way a chatbot can be. In dealing with computer code in particular, or learning new skills, AI is quite effective. (I would argue, as a teacher, that it is still unable to write a convincing and original argument, though.)

That said:

Neither ChatGPT, Gemini, nor Claude have consciousness, nor likely will they. According to Hilary Putnam, there is a distinction between creation and meaning. In Reason, Truth, and History, in the chapter where he gives his famous "Brain in a Vat" thought experiment, Putnam opens with a hypothetical scenario in which an ant accidentally traces an outline in the sand of the face of Winston Churchill. The image, in a sense, "is" one of Winston Churchill; however, the ant did not intend it, did not mean it. It is possible, in this same way, for chatbots to say things but not mean things. It is this very distinction that shows the flaw in the Turing Test. According to the Turing Test, a machine would be conscious if it could fool a human into thinking it was conscious; however, this is plainly a flawed metric, since, for example, an infinite number of monkeys banging away at an infinite number of typewriters might eventually write Hamlet, and, by Turing's logic, we would have to admit that the monkey that banged out Hamlet was in fact Shakespeare.

From one angle, the argument that chatbots are conscious betrays a flaw in the way people think of human beings as learning and thinking. They think that the process of training a chatbot is analogous, theoretically, to the development of a human being. They'll say, perhaps, that learning to speak is just copying what somebody else says and repeating it at appropriate moments. This, they might reason, is exactly what large-language models do: They study patterns of speech and regurgitate them in a believable order. We could say the same for visual art. Because there are, technically speaking, no entirely original ideas in art, and all art is to some extent "copying," then there is no difference between what a human does and what generative AI does when painting a picture.

We see from these arguments that the issue is not in what these AI advocates claim AI can do, but in the poverty of their perspective on what humans do.

Part of this poverty arises from flawed theories inherited from the early-modern period. Theorists like Locke and Hobbes in their perceptual arguments have largely been superseded by Phenomenologist philosophers like Husserl and Merleau-Ponty. And yet we remain the sons and daughters of Locke. According to this particular, British-empiricist viewpoint, consciousness amounts to sense data; we know nothing, are nothing, and then the data enters through the senses. For this reason it might be reasonable to assume that computers could at some point be conscious. However, this theory runs into numerous problems, the first being how it deals with disability. If a person is blind, are they literally less conscious? It is implausible that most of us would agree with those sort of statement.

The Phenomenologists, to remedy the problems introduced by sense-data theories of perception, devised a notion of consciousness based upon relationships, namely intentionality. We do not perceive objects because we see them or feel them but because we have intentional relationships with them. We perceive a chair because we have an intentional relationship with that chair that involves sitting. If one was blind, that intentional relationship would still remain. If, however, one was differently abled and could not sit in the chair, their perception of the chair would not be lessened but the character of their relationship with it would be changed.

If, therefore, intentional relationships with objects are necessary for consciousness, then it would follow that neither ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude are conscious, because they can say things, like the ant draws things, but they are unable to intend things, and it is intention that is necessary for consciousness.

Of course, this argument amounts to proving a negative, something that is famously difficult to do, if not impossible. However, these are reasons to be highly skeptical that a machine could be conscious based on current LLM technology. Perhaps one would argue that an AI agent could have an intentional relationship with some digital object, but I would still argue that the core of the structure of the technology is merely repetition and copying, and this is not enough to imply intention.

If these chatbots are conscious, it is the purview of those making that bombastic claim to prove it based on an actual theory of consciousness.
